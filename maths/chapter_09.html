Chapter Summary

LINES AND ANGLES

This chapter introduces the fundamental concepts of geometry involving lines, rays, and angles, focusing on their relationships and properties when lines intersect or run parallel.

Learning Objectives

  • Recognise and define complementary angles, supplementary angles, adjacent angles, linear pair and vertically opposite angles.
  • Recognise and define a transversal.
  • Recognise and explain angles formed by a transversal that intersects two or more lines.
  • State the properties of angles formed when a transversal intersects two or more parallel lines.

Tune Up

This section revises basic geometric elements learned in previous classes.

  • Point ‚ÄĒ A dot on a plane with no thickness, denoted by a capital letter such as A, B, P, Q.
  • Line segment ‚ÄĒ A part of a line with two end points.
  • Line ‚ÄĒ A straight path extending endlessly in both directions with no end points.
  • Ray ‚ÄĒ A part of a line having one end point (starting point) and extending endlessly in one direction.
  • Angle ‚ÄĒ Formed when two lines, line segments, or rays meet at a common point called the vertex. The rays are called arms.

SPECIAL PAIRS OF ANGLES

A combination of two angles is called a pair of angles. Certain pairs have special properties based on their relationship.

Complementary Angles

Two angles are said to be complementary if the sum of their measures is 90¬į. Each angle is said to be a complement of the other.

  • Complementary Angles ‚ÄĒ Two angles whose measures add up to 90¬į.
  • Complement ‚ÄĒ One angle is the complement of the other if their sum is 90¬į.
  • Formula: If x is an angle, its complement is 90¬į - x.
  • Example: 45¬į and 45¬į are complementary; 60¬į and 30¬į are complementary.

Supplementary Angles

Two angles are said to be supplementary if the sum of their measures is 180¬į. Each angle is said to be a supplement of the other.

  • Supplementary Angles ‚ÄĒ Two angles whose measures add up to 180¬į.
  • Supplement ‚ÄĒ One angle is the supplement of the other if their sum is 180¬į.
  • Formula: If x is an angle, its supplement is 180¬į - x.
  • Example: 120¬į and 60¬į are supplementary; 150¬į and 30¬į are supplementary.

Grasp It

  • Complement of x = 90¬į - x.
  • Supplement of x = 180¬į - x.
  • These formulas help calculate the missing angle in a complementary or supplementary pair.

Adjacent Angles

Two angles are said to be adjacent if they have a common vertex, a common arm, and the other two arms lie on opposite sides of the common arm.

  • Adjacent Angles ‚ÄĒ Angles sharing a common vertex and a common arm.
  • Non-common arms must lie on opposite sides of the common arm.
  • Example: In a figure where angle AOB and angle BOC share ray OB, they are adjacent.
  • Angles sharing a vertex but lying on the same side of the common arm are not adjacent.

Linear Pair

A pair of adjacent angles is said to form a linear pair if the non-common arms are opposite rays and the angles are supplementary.

  • Linear Pair ‚ÄĒ A pair of adjacent angles forming a straight line.
  • Non-common arms are opposite rays.
  • Angles in a linear pair are always supplementary (sum is 180¬į).
  • All linear pairs are adjacent, but not all adjacent angles form a linear pair.

Angles on a Straight Line

The sum of angles formed on a straight line is 180¬į.

  • When multiple angles lie on a straight line, their total measure equals 180¬į.
  • This is derived from the fact that angles on a straight line form a linear pair.
  • Example: If angles 1, 2, 3, 4, 5 are on a line, then angle 1 + angle 2 + angle 3 + angle 4 + angle 5 = 180¬į.

Vertically Opposite Angles

When two lines intersect each other, each pair of angles formed without any common arm is called vertically opposite angles.

  • Vertically Opposite Angles ‚ÄĒ Angles opposite to each other when two lines intersect.
  • They have no common arm.
  • Vertically opposite angles are always equal to each other.
  • Proof involves using the linear pair property to show equality.

TRANSVERSAL

The word 'transverse' means 'to place across'. A transversal is a line which intersects two or more lines at different points.

  • Transversal ‚ÄĒ A line that cuts across two or more other lines at distinct points.
  • If a line cuts two lines at the same point, it is not considered a transversal for those lines.
  • A transversal creates eight angles when intersecting two lines.

Angles Formed by a Transversal

When a transversal intersects two lines at different points, eight angles are formed.

1. Interior angles

All angles which lie between the two lines being intersected are called interior angles.

  • Interior angles ‚ÄĒ Angles located between the two lines cut by the transversal.
  • There are four interior angles in total.
  • Example: Angles 3, 4, 5, and 6 in standard diagrams.

2. Exterior angles

All angles which do not have the line segment between the intersection points as one of their arms are called exterior angles.

  • Exterior angles ‚ÄĒ Angles located outside the two lines cut by the transversal.
  • There are four exterior angles in total.
  • Example: Angles 1, 2, 7, and 8 in standard diagrams.

3. Alternate interior angles

The interior angles which lie on the opposite sides of the transversal and do not form a linear pair are called alternate interior angles.

  • Alternate interior angles ‚ÄĒ Interior angles on opposite sides of the transversal.
  • They form a 'Z' shape.
  • Example: Angle 4 and Angle 6; Angle 3 and Angle 5.

4. Alternate exterior angles

The exterior angles which lie on the opposite sides of the transversal and do not form a linear pair are called alternate exterior angles.

  • Alternate exterior angles ‚ÄĒ Exterior angles on opposite sides of the transversal.
  • Example: Angle 1 and Angle 7; Angle 2 and Angle 8.

5. Corresponding angles

A pair of angles on the same side of the transversal, one of which is an interior angle and the other one is an exterior angle, but do not form a linear pair, is known as a pair of corresponding angles.

  • Corresponding angles ‚ÄĒ Angles in matching corners relative to the intersection points.
  • They form an 'F' shape.
  • Example: Angle 1 and Angle 5; Angle 2 and Angle 6; Angle 4 and Angle 8; Angle 3 and Angle 7.

6. Co-interior angles

A pair of interior angles on the same side of the transversal is called co-interior angles.

  • Co-interior angles ‚ÄĒ Interior angles on the same side of the transversal.
  • Also known as consecutive interior angles.
  • Example: Angle 4 and Angle 5; Angle 3 and Angle 6.

Angles Formed by a Transversal with Parallel Lines

Parallel lines are lines that do not intersect each other. When a transversal intersects two parallel lines, the eight angles formed have special properties.

  • Parallel lines ‚ÄĒ Lines that never meet and are equidistant throughout.
  • Alternate interior angles are equal.
  • Alternate exterior angles are equal.
  • Corresponding angles are equal.
  • Co-interior angles are supplementary (sum is 180¬į).

Converse of the property

Conversely, if any one of the above three given conditions is satisfied when two lines are intersected by a transversal, then the two lines must be parallel to each other.

  • If alternate angles are equal, lines are parallel.
  • If corresponding angles are equal, lines are parallel.
  • If co-interior angles sum to 180¬į, lines are parallel.

Grasp It

  • When parallel lines are intersected by a transversal, the shape of 'F' shows corresponding angles.
  • The shape of 'Z' shows alternate interior angles.
  • These visual shapes help identify angle pairs quickly.

# SPECIAL PAIRS OF ANGLES

A combination of two angles is called a pair of angles. Certain pairs of angles have special properties based on their relationship. Let us study about these special pairs of angles.

## Complementary Angles

Two angles are said to be complementary if the sum of their measures is 90¬į. Each angle is said to be a complement of the other.

[IMAGE_START] Two diagrams showing complementary angles. In diagram a), ‚ą†AOB and ‚ą†BOC are complementary angles with measures 45¬į and 45¬į respectively. In diagram b), ‚ą†PQR and ‚ą†XYZ are complementary angles with measures 60¬į and 30¬į respectively. [IMAGE_END]

## Supplementary Angles

Two angles are said to be supplementary if the sum of their measures is 180¬į. Each angle is said to be a supplement of the other.

[IMAGE_START] Two diagrams showing supplementary angles. In diagram a), ‚ą†AOB and ‚ą†BOC are supplementary angles with measures 120¬į and 60¬į respectively. In diagram b), ‚ą†PQR and ‚ą†XYZ are supplementary angles with measures 150¬į and 30¬į respectively. [IMAGE_END]

## Grasp It

If \( x \) is any angle, then
- complement of \( x = 90¬į - x \).
- supplement of \( x = 180¬į - x \).

## Solved Examples

### Example 1

The measure of an angle is equal to its complement. Find the measure of the angle.

Solution: Let the angle be \( x \).

Complement of \( x = 90¬į - x \)

It is given that \( x \) is equal to its complement.

\[ x = 90¬į - x \]

\[ \Rightarrow 2x = 90¬į \]

\[ \Rightarrow x = \frac{90¬į}{2} = 45¬į \]

Thus, the required angle is 45¬į.

### Example 2

In a pair of supplementary angles, the greater angle is 54¬į more than the smaller angle. Find the measure of the angles.

Solution: Let the greater angle be \( x \) and the smaller angle be \( (180¬į - x) \).

According to the given condition,

\[ x - (180¬į - x) = 54¬į \]

\[ \Rightarrow x - 180¬į + x = 54¬į \]

\[ \Rightarrow 2x = 54¬į + 180¬į \]

\[ \Rightarrow 2x = 234¬į \]

\[ \Rightarrow x = 117¬į \]

Thus, the required angles are 117¬į and \( (180¬į - 117¬į) = 63¬į \).

## Adjacent Angles

Two angles are said to be adjacent, if they have a common vertex, a common arm and the other two arms lie on opposite sides of the common arm.

## Linear Pair

A pair of adjacent angles is said to form a linear pair, if the non-common arms are opposite rays and the angles are supplementary.

[IMAGE_START] Diagram showing two adjacent angles forming a linear pair with the non-common arms as opposite rays. [IMAGE_END]

## Grasp It

- Linear pair of angles are always adjacent.
- Adjacent angles may or may not form a linear pair.
- Linear pair of angles are always supplementary.

# TRANSVERSAL

The word 'transverse' means 'to place across'. A transversal is a line which intersects (goes across) two or more lines at different points.

[IMAGE_START] Diagram showing two lines AB and CD intersected by a transversal EF at points P and Q. Angles are labeled as ‚ą†1, ‚ą†2, ‚ą†3, ‚ą†4, ‚ą†5, ‚ą†6, ‚ą†7, and ‚ą†8. [IMAGE_END]

In Fig. 9.17 a), EF is a transversal to the lines AB and CD because it intersects them at different points P and Q, respectively. But in Fig. 9.17 b), EF is not a transversal because it cuts both the lines AB and CD at the same point P.

## Angles Formed by a Transversal

When a transversal intersects two lines at different points, then eight angles are formed as shown in Fig. 9.18. Let us study about these angles in detail.

[IMAGE_START] Diagram showing two lines AB and CD intersected by a transversal EF at points G and H. Angles are labeled as ‚ą†1, ‚ą†2, ‚ą†3, ‚ą†4, ‚ą†5, ‚ą†6, ‚ą†7, and ‚ą†8. [IMAGE_END]

### 1. Interior angles

All angles which have the line segment GH as one arm are called interior angles; they lie between lines AB and CD. In Fig. 9.18, ‚ą†3, ‚ą†4, ‚ą†5 and ‚ą†6 are interior angles.

### 2. Exterior angles

All angles which do not have the line segment GH as one of their arms are called exterior angles, as they lie outside the lines AB and CD.

### 3. Alternate interior angles

The interior angles which lie on the opposite sides of the transversal and do not form a linear pair are called alternate interior angles. In Fig. 9.18, the two pairs of alternate interior angles are ‚ą†4 and ‚ą†6, and ‚ą†3 and ‚ą†5.

### 4. Alternate exterior angles

The exterior angles which lie on the opposite sides of the transversal and do not form a linear pair are called alternate exterior angles. In Fig. 9.18, the two pairs of alternate exterior angles are ‚ą†1 and ‚ą†7, and ‚ą†2 and ‚ą†8.

### 5. Corresponding angles

A pair of angles on the same side of the transversal, one of which is an interior angle and the other one is an exterior angle, but do not form a linear pair, is known as a pair of corresponding angles. In Fig. 9.18, the pairs of corresponding angles are ‚ą†1 and ‚ą†5, ‚ą†2 and ‚ą†6, ‚ą†4 and ‚ą†8, and ‚ą†3 and ‚ą†7.

### 6. Co-interior angles

A pair of interior angles on the same side of the transversal is called co-interior angles. In Fig. 9.18, ‚ą†4 and ‚ą†5, and ‚ą†3 and ‚ą†6 are pairs of co-interior angles.

## Angles Formed by a Transversal with Parallel Lines

We have already learnt about parallel lines in previous classes. Parallel lines are the lines that do not intersect each other.

When a transversal intersects two parallel lines, the eight angles that are formed have special properties in relation to each other. In Fig. 9.19, two parallel lines AB and CD, intersected by a transversal EF at points G and H, form eight

# Angles

Let us learn the special properties associated with these angles.

1. $\angle 3 = \angle 5$ and $\angle 4 = \angle 6$
   - Alternate interior angles are equal.

2. $\angle 2 = \angle 8$ and $\angle 1 = \angle 7$
   - Alternate exterior angles are equal.

3. $\angle 1 = \angle 5$, $\angle 2 = \angle 6$, $\angle 4 = \angle 8$ and $\angle 3 = \angle 7$
   - Corresponding angles are equal.

4. $\angle 4 + \angle 5 = 180^\circ$ and $\angle 3 + \angle 6 = 180^\circ$
   - Co-interior angles are supplementary.

The above properties can be verified by measuring the angle using a protractor.

Thus, we can conclude that when parallel lines are intersected by a transversal, then
1. the alternate angles are equal.
2. the corresponding angles are equal.
3. the sum of co-interior angles is $180^\circ$.

## Converse of the property

Conversely, if any one of the above three given conditions is satisfied when two lines are intersected by a transversal, then the two lines must be parallel to each other.

When the parallel lines are intersected by a transversal, the shape of 'F' is formed, which shows the pairs of corresponding angles, and the shape of 'Z' shows the pairs of alternate interior angles.

# Mental Maths

## Understand

- The sum of two complementary angles is 90¬į and that of two supplementary angles is 180¬į.
- Vertically opposite angles are equal to each other.
- Two angles form a linear pair if they are adjacent and supplementary.
- When a transversal cuts two lines, pairs of corresponding angles, alternate (exterior and interior) angles and interior angles on the same side of the transversal are formed.
